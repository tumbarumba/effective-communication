Thinking Systems

  • System 2: deliberative, logical, effortful, lazy
  • Most of what you think and do originates in System 1 with System 2 called upon to prevent errors

Cognitive Biases

  • There are hundreds of them!
  • For example...
    • Illusion of asymmetric insight
    • Illusion of transparency
    • Fundamental attribution error
    • Confirmation bias
  • Asymmetric insight: I know more about you than you know about me
  • Transparency: my feelings are obvious to you, your feelings are obvious to me
  • Fundamental attribution:
    • personal successes are due to good character
    • personal failures are due to bad luck
    • other people's successes are due to good luck
    • other people's failures are due to character flaws
  • Confirmation: you notice stuff you believe in, ignore the stuff you don't
  • Check out the Wikipedia page List of Cognitive Biases"
  • Book and Podcast: You Are Not So Smart

Action Science

  • Research of Chris Agyris between 1957 and 2004
  • The study of how human beings design their actions in difficult situations
  • Feedback loops based on ladder of inference, double loop learning
  • ...
  • Example:
    • Observation: one committee member is looking at his phone and not contributing to the meeting
    • Add meaning: when someone doesn't contribute it means that they don't care
    • Assumptions: if people don't care, they shouldn't be invited to future meetings
    • Actions: I will not invite Joe to future meetings
  • This is a coherent story when can easily think, therefore we quickly jump to the assumption that it is true.
  • Coherence busting to the rescue!

Agyris's Models

  • Unilateral Control Model
  • Mutual Learning Model
  • Espoused theory versus theory in use

Unilateral Control Model

Values

  • Win, don’t lose
  • Be right
  • Minimise expressions of negative feelings
  • Act rational
Assumptions:
  • I understand the situation; those who disagree don’t
  • I am right; those who disagree are wrong
  • I have pure motives; those who disagree have questionable motives
  • My feelings and behaviour are justified
  • I am not contributing to the problem

Mutual Learning Model

Values

  • Transparency
  • Curiosity
  • Informed choice
  • Accountability
  • Compassion
Assumptions:
  • I have information; so do other people
  • Each of us sees things others don’t
  • People may disagree with me and still have pure motives
  • Differences are opportunities for learning
  • I may be contributing to the problem

Unilateral Control Model

Outcomes

  • Ineffective working relationships
  • Lower performance
  • Reduced well-being
  • Relationships: Lower commitment, Decreased trust, Reduced learning, Greater defensiveness, Unproductive conflict, Inappropriate dependence on others
  • Performance: Lower-quality decisions, Less innovation, Longer implementation time, Increased costs
  • Individual Well-Being: Reduced motivation, Decreased satisfaction, Limited development opportunities, Increased stress

Mutual Learning Model

Outcomes

  • Effective working relationships
  • Improved performance
  • Improved well-being
  • Relationships: Greater commitment, Increased trust, Increased learning, Reduced defensiveness, Productive conflict, Appropriate dependence on others
  • Performance: Higher-quality decisions, Greater innovation, Shorter implementation time, Reduced costs
  • Individual Well-Being: Increased motivation, Increased satisfaction, Richer development opportunities, Reduced stress

8 Behaviours for Mutual Learning

  • Whitepaper by Roger Schwarz: 8 Behaviors for Leading a Smarter Team

Behaviour 1

State views and ask genuine questions

  • e.g. I think it would be helpful if we started with 2-3 key customers so that we can see how it works and eliminate any problems before they come up. What are your thoughts about starting with a small group?
  • You question needs to be genuine. e.g. "Do you really think that will work?" is not genuine, because it has an embedded assumption that you think it won't work.
  • You can convert it to a genuine question by stating your views first.

Behaviour 2

Share all relevant information

  • Present all inforamtion that you are aware of that will affect how a team solves a problem or makes a decision.
  • If everyone pools their knowledge, then the everyone will feel that they have made a fully informed choice, and will feel accountable to the decision
  • If someone later reveals information that they knew that contradicts the decision, trust will be reduced.

Behaviour 3

Use specific examples and agree on what important words mean

  • "deploy"?
  • "production incident"?
  • "meet at the usual place"?

Behaviour 4

Explain reasoning and intent

  • "I'm taking you off project X to work on strategy"
  • Without context or background, we will make up stories (coherence, system 1)
  • These stories often distorted and based on fears.
  • e.g. "I'm underperforming, and I'm being demoted"
  • Be transparent about what you are thinking, and why.
  • "I'm really concerned about that our upcoming projects are off base. I need some safe hands I trust that will set us up in a really good position for next year. Can you help with the strategy?"

Behaviour 5

Focus on interests, not positions

  • "Getting to Yes", by Roger Fisher and William Ury (published 1981)
  • Focusing steps:
    • Identify interests. Ask: "No matter what the solution is, we need to do this in a way that..."
    • Clarify and agree on interests. Agree on what important words mean
    • Generate potential solutions that meet the interests. Generative, design thinking
    • Select a solution and implement it. Real options. Fast experiments.

Behaviour 6

Test assumptions and inferences

  • Remember the ladder of inference.
  • e.g. "You're doing a great job, but the project has been slowing your team down. I'm going to give the new project to Brenda's team"
  • What did she really mean? Why is she saying that? You will think of a story that explains it (system 1)
  • If the story is a negative one, you will respond in a negative way.
  • If you test the validity of this story, you practice transparency and accountability with your thinking, and reveal a curiosity about what others think.

Behaviour 7

Jointly design next steps

  • Given your view and given my view, what sort of experiement should we run?
  • Turn the Ship Around, by L. David Marquet. Turning followers into leaders

Behaviour 8

Discuss undiscussable issues

  • We often believe that we can't tell people what we really think
  • Instead, we ask questions that imply what we mean
  • People understand you're not being clear, and lose trust
  • e.g Team of 5: Heather, Carlos, Stan, Lynn, Jim. Heather, Carlos and Stan think that Jim and Heather are underperforming. If they jointly believe that, but don't raise the issue, trust and accountability is reduced. Jim and Heather will know that they are not trusted, and disengage. By raising the issue with the team, everyone can verify their assumptions and agree on a plan of action (accountability).
