    Cognitive Biases

    • Illusion of asymmetric insight
    • Illusion of transparency
    • Self-serving bias
    • Confirmation bias
    • ... and hundreds more...
    • Asymmetric insight: I know more about you than you know about me
    • Transparency: my feelings are obvious to you, your feelings are obvious to me
    • Self-serving: successes are due to me, failures are other people's fault
    • Confirmation: you notice stuff you believe in, ignore the stuff you don't

    Thinking, Fast and Slow

    Daniel Kahneman

    • System 1: fast, involuntary, no effort, biased
    • System 2: slow, voluntary, effortful, lazy
    • Based on original research, often with collaboration with Amos Tversky
    • System 2 is what we think of as our "self", the voice in our head
    • System 1 has biases, which system 2 can correct, but also has different biases
    • WYSIATI (what you see is all there is)
    • Availability heuristic: if you can easily think of it, it's probably true
    • Coherence
    • What is the woman feeling?
    • What is she going to do next?
    • Without effort, your brain has probably supplied you with a story about both of those questions
    • The story is probably not true, but we will act as if it is true

    Action Science

    • Research of Chris Agyris between 1957 and 2004
    • The study of how human beings design their actions in difficult situations
    • Feedback loops based on ladder of inference, double loop learning
    • Example:
      • Observation: one committee member is looking at his phone and not contributing to the meeting
      • Add meaning: when someone doesn't contribute it means that they don't care
      • Assumptions: if people don't care, they shouldn't be invited to future meetings
      • Actions: I will not invite Joe to future meetings
    • This is a coherent story when can easily think, therefore we quickly jump to the assumption that it is true.
    • Coherence busting to the rescue!

    Agyris's Models

    • Unilateral Control Model
    • Mutual Learning Model
    • Espoused theory versus theory in use

    Unilateral Control: Values

    • Win, don’t lose
    • Be right
    • Minimise expressions of negative feelings
    • Act rational
    Assumptions:
    • I understand the situation; those who disagree don’t
    • I am right; those who disagree are wrong
    • I have pure motives; those who disagree have questionable motives
    • My feelings and behaviour are justified
    • I am not contributing to the problem

    Unilateral Control: Outcomes

    • Ineffective working relationships
    • Lower performance
    • Reduced well-being
    • Relationships: Lower commitment, Decreased trust, Reduced learning, Greater defensiveness, Unproductive conflict, Inappropriate dependence on others
    • Performance: Lower-quality decisions, Less innovation, Longer implementation time, Increased costs
    • Individual Well-Being: Reduced motivation, Decreased satisfaction, Limited development opportunities, Increased stress
    • Theory X

    Mutual Learning: Values

    • Transparency
    • Curiosity
    • Informed choice
    • Accountability
    • Compassion
    Assumptions:
    • I have information; so do other people
    • Each of us sees things others don’t
    • People may disagree with me and still have pure motives
    • Differences are opportunities for learning
    • I may be contributing to the problem

    Mutual Learning: Outcomes

    • Effective working relationships
    • Improved performance
    • Improved well-being
    • Relationships: Greater commitment, Increased trust, Increased learning, Reduced defensiveness, Productive conflict, Appropriate dependence on others
    • Performance: Higher-quality decisions, Greater innovation, Shorter implementation time, Reduced costs
    • Individual Well-Being: Increased motivation, Increased satisfaction, Richer development opportunities, Reduced stress

    Mutual Learning Behaviours

    1. State views and ask genuine questions
    2. Share all relevant information
    3. Use specific examples and agree exact terms
    4. Explain reasoning and intent
    5. Focus on interests, not positions
    6. Test assumptions and inferences
    7. Jointly design next steps
    8. Discuss undiscussable issues

    Non Violent Communication (NVC)

    • Dr Marshall Rosenberg
    • People attempts to meet their needs
    • Conflict arises when when strategies for meeting needs clash
    • Shared needs are identifed by the thoughts and feelings arising from the needs
    • 3 levels: within self, between others, and within groups
    • NVC strategy:
      • Identify shared needs
      • Collaborate to satisfy needs

    Violent Communication

    • Moralistic judgements
    • Demands
    • Denial of responsiblity
    • Making comparisons
    • Assessment of deserving punishment or reward
    • Coercive or manipulative language that aims to induce guilt, fear, shame
    • Moralistic judgements: blame, insults, put-downs, labels, criticisms, comparisons
    • Demands: implicitly or explicitly threaten blame or punishment
    • Denial of responsibility: "I had to", "Someone else make me", group pressure, rules, uncontrollable impulses

    Components of NVC

    • Observation
    • Feelings
    • Needs
    • Request
    • Observation: the facts, what we are seeing, hearing, touching. Free of evaluation, meaning or significance
    • Feelings: emotions or sensations, free of thought or story
    • Needs: universal human needs, such as protection, affection, understanding, identity, etc
    • Request: request for a specific action, free of demand
    • e.g when you walked in the room and didn't acknowlege me, I felt hurt and insulted. I need to feel that I'm acknowleged and respected. Could you please say "Hello" to me when you come in?

    Receiving Empathically
